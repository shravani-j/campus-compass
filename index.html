First Month Timeline

Week 1: Orientation & Settling In

You'll arrive, get your hostel room, and sit through long speeches in the auditorium. Everything will feel overwhelming and crowded.

Senior Objective:

Just get comfortable. Find out where your classrooms are, save your hostel warden's number, and introduce yourself to the person sitting next to you.

Week 2: Classes Begin

The academic routine kicks in. Labs start, textbooks/slides are introduced, and teachers outline their course objectives and policies.

Senior Objective:

Figure out the lab schedule. Buy an extension box if you haven't. Get into the habit of waking up early enough to catch breakfast before 8:45 AM.

Week 3: Club Exposure

Clubs start advertising, putting up booths, and calling for registrations. Senior interactions pick up pace.

Senior Objective:

Register for clubs you like. Don't worry about skills yet; clubs look for enthusiasm, curiosity, and willingness to learn. Attend the info sessions!

Week 4: First Reality Check :)

Assignments start piling up, and the first set of class tests or quizzes might be announced. The honeymoon phase ends.

Senior Objective:

Keep track of submissions on a calendar. Ensure your attendance is healthy (75%+). Do not start falling behind now, it accumulates fast!

02 / Academics

Academics: Study smart, not scared.

Let's demystify branches, grading structures, class timetables, and attendance. It's not rocket science, but if you ignore the basics, it catches up quickly.

B.Tech Branches at Amaravati

Here are the main branches you guys are enrolled in. The first year curriculum is largely shared, but your specialization defines your core lab courses later.

Computer Science (Core)

Focuses on computer architecture, systems, databases, and general software development.

AI & Machine Learning

Heavy emphasis on mathematics, algorithms, data modeling, and neural networks.

Computer Communication

Blends networks, communication systems, cloud infrastructures, and system protocols.

AI & Data Science

Focuses on analyzing complex data, statistics, visual modeling, and pipeline development.

CSE (Quantum Computing)

A specialized program combining core computer science with quantum computing, quantum algorithms, and emerging technologies.

Electronics and Communication Engineering

Focuses on electronics, communication systems, embedded devices, signal processing, and hardware-software integration.

Timings & Routines

Classes generally begin promptly at 8:55 AM. Keep this in mind when scheduling alarm clocks!

  • End Times: May vary daily. Some days you might finish at 2:00 PM, others at 3:00 PM, 4:00 PM, or sometimes 5:00 PM.
  • Labs: There is generally one lab session (typically lasting 2-3 hours) almost every day. Don't skip these; labs carry high internal marks.
  • Breaks: Lunch breaks are typically 1 hour, staggered depending on your branch section.

Crucial Will I survive attendance?

Amrita is strict about this. A minimum of 75% attendance is mandatory for every single course.

Why it actually matters:

  • Exam Eligibility: If your attendance drops below 75%, you will be barred from writing the End Semester Exam (ESE) for that course. No exceptions.
  • Outpass Approval: Low attendance can make warden/hostel office reject your homepass/outpass approvals. They track your class presence closely.
  • Overall Discipline: Regular attendance builds a rapport with faculty, which goes a long way when requesting help later.

CIA + ESE Structure

Your grade in a subject is divided between continuous assessment and the final exam.

CIA (Continuous Internal Assessment)

Typically 50% of your total marks. Includes assignments, class tests (Internals), lab practicals, quizzes, and class participation.

Treat internals as free marks!

ESE (End Semester Examination)

Typically 50% of total marks. A comprehensive exam covering the full syllabus, sometimes including separate lab exams.

50 or 100 mark exams
Relative Grading Note: SGPA is calculated relatively. Your grade depends on your performance compared to the rest of the batch. If the paper is tough, it's tough for everyone!

Your CGPA matters. But it is not your personality.

Maintaining a healthy CGPA keeps options open for placements, master's programs, and research. Here is the realistic target and how to achieve it.

Target: 8.5+ CGPA

This keeps you in the top bracket, makes you eligible for any recruitment drive, and leaves a buffer for tougher upper-year courses.

Treat Internals as Gold

It is much easier to score 45/50 in internals (assignments + tests) than to salvage a grade by studying all night for a 100-mark final.

Ask Seniors & PYQs

Previous Year Questions (PYQs) are cheat codes. Professors often repeat question formats. Ask seniors for notes and resources.

Personal Advice:

Score well in the first four semesters. It builds a solid foundation. In later semesters, project loads, internships, and club activities demand heavy attention, making it much harder to pull up a low CGPA. Do not slack in year one!

But please don't make CGPA your entire identity.

Stay active. Play. Dance. Code. Compete. Write. Show up.

This place slowly starts feeling like home.

Hostel life takes a little getting used to, but it quickly becomes one of the most memorable parts of college. Between roommates, late-night conversations, mess food debates, and shared experiences, you'll build friendships that often last well beyond graduation.

Hostel Timings

  • Gates usually open around 6:00 AM, so you can go for a morning walk, run, or play your sport before the day starts.
  • Students are generally allowed outside the hostel premises only until around 8:00 PM.
  • If you've ordered food or are collecting deliveries, you may be allowed additional time, typically until around 8:45 PM.

Outpasses & Homepasses

Before you plan a trip home, there are a few rules worth knowing:

  • Apply at least 24 hours in advance
  • Emergency approvals are uncommon and not guaranteed
  • Parent approval is required for homepasses and long day-passes
  • Applications are processed through My Amrita
  • Specify departure and return details
  • ID card swipes are required while leaving and returning
  • Entry and exit times are logged
Important: Don't wait until the last minute. Pass approvals are much smoother when applied for in advance.

First Week Survival Guide

  • Learn your route to class: Do a mock walk to your classroom building the evening before classes begin to avoid panic at 8:50 AM.
  • Sit with different people: Everyone is looking for friends. Sitting with random classmates in Week 1 is the easiest way to break the ice.
  • Save important contacts: Save numbers of the hostel warden, security gate, hostel office, and at least two class classmates.
  • Fix your sleep schedule: Mess closes early, and classes start at 8:55 AM sharp. Transitioning from late night chats takes time.
  • Attend orientation properly: While long, it answers practical doubts about portals, exams, and grading.
  • Don't isolate yourself: Keep your room doors slightly open when inside during first few days, talk to wing-mates, go down for evening snacks.

05 / Survival Kit

Small things that save your semester.

A curated list of dos, don'ts, mistakes, time management advice, and mental wellbeing checks straight from seniors who learned them the hard way.

DOs

  • Always carry your ID card: Security won't let you cross the main gate, hostels, or mess halls without it.
  • Sleep properly: All-nighters right before tests rarely work. Consistent 6 hours sleep is a cheat code.
  • Submit work early: Server crashes on assignment submission deadlines are real. Upload assignments at least 4 hours before.
  • Ask doubts in labs: Lab teachers are very helpful if you show curiosity. It also leaves a great impression for internal marks.
  • Use calendar tools: Track test schedules, club recruitment deadlines, and events using Google Calendar.
  • Talk to seniors: We have the notes, the past question papers, and the advice on which electives to pick. Don't be shy!

DON'Ts

  • Skip classes casually: Taking a day off feels fun, but bunking three classes in a row can drop your attendance below 75% before you know it.
  • Compare yourself constantly: Some classmates will code like experts, others will speak flawlessly. Everyone has different paces. Focus on your growth.
  • Wait until exam week: The syllabus is huge. Trying to cram 5 units of Engineering Physics or Calculus the night before is a recipe for disaster.
  • Overcommit to clubs: Don't join 4 clubs. You'll end up neglecting all of them and your CGPA. Join 1 or max 2 active clubs.

Mistakes Seniors Made

  • Trying to do everything: Coding, sports, cultural events, fests, and GPA all at once. Pick your battles.
  • Ignoring attendance early: Thinking "I'll make up for it later." It is very hard to pull attendance back up in the final months.
  • Not asking for help: Struggling alone with coding labs or math concepts. Teachers and seniors are super approachable if you ask.
  • Studying only before exams: Resulted in unnecessary anxiety and bad grades. Just 30 mins review daily is enough.
  • Skipping opportunities: Not applying to hackathons or fests because of fear of failure. Start early!

A Reminder: Bad weeks happen.

You might fail a class quiz. You might argue with a roommate. You might feel incredibly homesick. All of this is part of growing up and living in college.

  • Take breaks: Take a short walk around campus when you need a break.
  • Talk to people: Don't lock yourself in your room. Reach out to friends or faculty mentors.
  • Focus on progress over perfection: A bad internal mark is just one quiz. You have plenty of opportunities to recover.

Your academic starter pack.

Skip the search. Here are the exact platforms, coding tools, note-taking apps, and college resources you should set up in your first week.

Learning

  • NPTEL

    Government platform for engineering courses. Often used for elective assignments and syllabus references.

  • GeeksforGeeks

    Perfect for understanding core computer science algorithms, theory, and coding lab codes.

  • LeetCode

    The standard platform for practicing coding logic. Vital for DSA preparation.

Development

  • VS Code

    The best code editor. Install it in Week 1. Add extensions for Python, C++, and Git.

  • GitHub

    Host your projects, version control your lab codes, and start building your developer profile.

  • Replit

    In-browser code editor. Great for quick coding practice without installing setups locally.

Organization

  • Notion

    Excellent tool for managing class notes, project sheets, schedules, and club works.

  • Google Keep

    Great for quick, cross-device checklist notes (like mess items or packing items).

  • Google Calendar

    Mark your internal exam dates, lab schedules, assignment deadlines, and project submissions.

Design & Projects

  • Figma

    Industry-standard UI/UX design tool. Perfect for coding projects and hackathon UI mocks.

  • Canva

    Useful for quick presentation slides, club poster designs, and resume formatting.

  • ChatGPT / Gemini

    Use it as a learning buddy to explain complex math equations or debugging errors, not to copy paste assignments.

College Portals

Bookmark these two. You will visit them almost every single day.

  • My Amrita Portal

    Your central student dashboard — attendance, fee payments, outpass approvals, hostel requests, notifications, and everything admin-related lives here.

  • AUMS — Academic Portal

    Academic records portal — mid-term marks, semester marks, SGPA, CGPA, grades, test links, evaluation forms, and course registration.
